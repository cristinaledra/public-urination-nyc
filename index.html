As NYC public restroom supply remains low, NYPD enforcement of public urination has spiked

 

By: Cristina Ledra     Nov. 25, 2024

 

Mayor Eric Adams' larger mission of improving quality of life for New York City residents includes enforcement of offenses like public urination.

The lack of available and well-maintained public bathrooms has been a problem in New York City for decades. While little progress has been made, enforcement of public urination laws has seen a spike under mayor Eric Adams.

Since he took office in 2022, Adams has championed quality of life efforts. And cracking down on such offenses like public urination is one of them. However, complaints to 311 about public urination have largely remained steady since 2018.

Adams announced the “Ur In Luck” initiative in June 2024 that aims to build 46 new public restrooms and renovate 36 others in the city over the next five years. He also introduced a Google Maps layer that allows anyone to view where the city’s public restrooms are located.

The Google Maps layer lists 931 public bathrooms in New York City. In a 2019 report by city comptroller Brad Lander, the 1,400 public bathrooms available then ranked New York City 93rd nationally in public restrooms per 100,000 residents. Around 400 of those bathrooms had some level of disrepair or inadequacy. While the top city on the list, St. Paul, Minn., had 210 public restrooms per 100,000 residents, New York City had just 16.

“The availability of public bathrooms has not increased significantly since the 1970s crisis years,” said Julie Chu of Urban Design Forum. “We believe the city has not truly recovered from this era, both in stigma around public bathrooms, but also in its commitment to truly rebuild the large network of public bathrooms it once had. Despite city population growth, economic growth, and millions of tourists visiting the city annually, bathroom conditions have changed very little.”

Although New York City once had a robust network of public bathrooms up until the 1930s, many fell into disrepair during the city’s budget crisis in the 1970s and became places where crime and disorder were common.

During her research, Chu found that other large cities around the world have come up with a number of solutions to make more public bathrooms available. Those include local governments paying private businesses to make their restrooms available to the public and installing stainless steel bathrooms that are more easily maintained than traditional public bathrooms. Some are even self-cleaning and would be available around the clock, unlike many public and private options which have opening and closing hours. Advertising could also be added to single stall bathrooms to help pay for operating costs.

In 2002, city council passed a bill requiring city officials to identify one location per zip code for a public restroom. But until there is at least one available and adequate public bathroom in every zip code in the city, New Yorkers and tourists alike are at a higher risk of being ticketed if they choose to relieve themselves in a public place without finding a bathroom.
